What is Andelsbolig (Danish Housing Cooperative)?

Denmark's cooperative apartment model — you buy a share (andel) in a housing cooperative giving the right to occupy a specific flat. Price-controlled by law based on the andelskronen calculation.

Key Facts — Andelsbolig (Danish Housing Cooperative)
  • Price: regulated by Andelsboligloven — cannot exceed andelskronen/valuation
  • Ownership: share in andelsboligforening, not freehold
  • Key document: andelskronen (share value) from association accounts
  • Faellesgjald: collective mortgage proportionally inherited
  • Financing: loans secured on the andel, not underlying real estate

An andelsbolig is a form of Danish cooperative housing where residents own shares (andele) in an andelsboligforening (housing cooperative) that entitles each member to the exclusive occupation of a specific apartment. The cooperative owns the underlying real estate. The price at which an andel can be sold is subject to strict statutory controls under the Danish Cooperative Housing Act (Andelsboligloven) — the seller cannot sell above the maximum regulated price.

The regulated price mechanism for andelsboliger was introduced to keep cooperative housing affordable and prevent speculative windfall gains. The maximum price is set as the lower of: the value calculated using the association's balance sheet method (andelskronen), the property's public assessment value, or an independent valuation by a licensed assessor.

Due diligence for andelsbolig purchases is more complex than for freehold apartments. Buyers must review the andelsboligforening's annual accounts (regnskab) to assess the andelskronen and financial health; check the underlying mortgage on the cooperative's building (faeellesgelad proportionally inherited); confirm the price does not exceed the legal maximum; and obtain confirmation from the association's administrator of membership approval.

Financing an andelsbolig is different from a standard mortgage. Danish banks lend against andelsboliger, but the loan is secured against the andel share rather than the underlying real estate. Loan-to-value ratios may be lower than for ejerlejligheder, and the specific cooperative's financial health affects lending decisions.

Foreign buyers unfamiliar with the andelsbolig system should proceed with caution. The price regulation, cooperative structure, and financing arrangements are complex. Engaging a Danish advokat experienced in andelsbolig transactions is essential.

Common Mistake: Buyers sometimes pay more than the legally permitted maximum price for an andelsbolig. An overpayment above the legal maximum can be challenged in court — the seller must return the excess. Always verify the maximum legal price with the association's administrator before agreeing terms.
Expert Tip: Request the andelsboligforening's most recent audited regnskab (annual accounts) and have a Danish advokat calculate the legal maximum purchase price before negotiating.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can foreigners buy andelsboliger in Denmark?

There are no nationality restrictions on purchasing andelsboliger for persons with Danish residence rights. The andelsboligforening's board must approve the new member. The cooperative's vedtaegter (statutes) set the membership criteria.

How is the andelskronen calculated?

The andelskronen is derived from the association's balance sheet: total assets (including property value) minus total liabilities (including collective mortgage), divided by total share capital. It changes annually as debt is paid down or property values change.

What is the difference between andelsbolig and ejerlejlighed in Denmark?

An ejerlejlighed is a freehold apartment — you own the unit outright and can sell at market price. An andelsbolig is a cooperative share — price controlled, financed differently, and subject to cooperative rules. Ejerlejligheder are generally more expensive but offer uncapped market upside.
