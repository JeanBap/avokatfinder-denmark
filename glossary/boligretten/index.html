Boligretten

Boligretten refers to Danish housing law — the body of legislation governing the rental, sale, and use of residential property in Denmark.

Danish housing law, known as boligretten, encompasses a wide set of statutes that regulate the rights and obligations of landlords, tenants, buyers, and sellers in the residential property market. The legal framework is designed to balance market efficiency with strong tenant protections.

Boligretten draws from several core acts including Lejeloven (the Rent Act), Boligreguleringsloven (the Housing Regulation Act), and the Andelsboligloven governing cooperative housing. Disputes arising from boligretten matters are often handled by the Huslejenævn (Rent Tribunal).

Boligretten is particularly important in rent-controlled municipalities such as Copenhagen, where rents for older properties are subject to strict caps. The municipality can designate a property's annual rent based on the 'cost rent' system, limiting increases.

For buyers, understanding boligretten means knowing which obligations transfer upon purchase of a tenanted property. Existing tenancies generally survive a sale, and Danish law provides significant security of tenure.

Common Mistake: Assuming Danish tenant protections are weak — in fact, evicting a tenant in Denmark for any reason other than breach of contract is extremely difficult and time-consuming.
Expert Tip: Always check whether a property you intend to buy is subject to rent regulation before completing the purchase, as this substantially affects the yield and exit options.

For international buyers acquiring tenanted Danish properties, boligretten creates significant obligations. Seeking specialist legal advice before purchase is strongly recommended.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the maximum deposit a landlord can charge in Denmark?

Under boligretten, a landlord may charge up to three months' rent as a security deposit and request up to three months' rent as prepaid rent, for a maximum upfront payment of six months' rent.

Can a landlord terminate a tenancy for personal use in Denmark?

Yes, but only under strict conditions defined in boligretten — the landlord must genuinely need the property for personal or close family use, and must give the required notice period, typically 12 months.

Where are housing law disputes resolved in Denmark?

Most rent disputes are handled by the local Huslejenævn (Rent Tribunal), a quasi-judicial body. Serious disputes or appeals can proceed to the ordinary courts.
