Ejendomsavancebeskatning

Ejendomsavancebeskatning Denmark – Capital Gains Tax on Property

Ejendomsavancebeskatning (property capital gains tax) is Denmark's tax on profits from selling real property. The rules are complex, with significant exemptions for owner-occupied primary and holiday residences that make most private home sales effectively tax-free. Understanding the exemptions is essential for planning any Danish property sale.

The key exemption is the parcel exemption (parcelhusreglen): gains from selling a property that has served as the owner's primary residence (or holiday home used exclusively by the household) are fully exempt from ejendomsavancebeskatning. There is no minimum ownership period — the exemption applies from the day the property becomes your primary residence.

For properties that do not qualify for the parcelhusregel exemption (typically investment properties), gains are calculated as: sale price minus acquisition cost minus documented improvement costs (not maintenance). The resulting gain is added to the taxpayer's personal income (personlig indkomst) and taxed at the marginal income tax rate, which can reach 52%+.

Key Facts

Primary residence exemptionParcelhusreglen — full exemption, no minimum period
Investment property rateAdded to personal income — up to 52%+
Cost base adjustmentsInflation indexation + documented improvements
Loss carry-forwardAgainst future property gains only
FilingEjendomsavanceopgørelse to SKAT in sale year

Losses on non-exempt property sales can be offset against gains on other taxable property disposals but cannot reduce other income types (unlike in some jurisdictions). Loss carry-forward is permitted indefinitely against future taxable property gains. The rules for aggregating gains and losses across multiple properties require careful accounting.

Sellers of investment properties must file an ejendomsavanceopgørelse (capital gains return) with SKAT (Danish Tax Agency) in the year of sale. The calculation uses a special indexation system (pristalsregulering) to adjust the historical cost base for inflation, which can significantly reduce the nominal taxable gain for long-held properties.

⚠ Common MistakeAssuming the parcelhusregel exemption applies automatically to a property that was once your home. If you rented it out for a period, the exemption may be partial — the ownership period as a rental property is potentially taxable.
💡 Expert TipKeep meticulous records of all capital improvements with original receipts from the moment of purchase. In Denmark, as in other Nordic countries, only documented costs reduce the taxable gain.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does the parcelhusregel apply to holiday homes?

Yes, but only if the holiday home was used exclusively by the owner's household. If it was rented to third parties even for short periods, the exemption may not apply.

How is inflation indexation applied?

SKAT applies an official index (pristalsindeks) to the acquisition cost each year of ownership. The indexed cost base reduces the nominal gain, which is particularly beneficial for long-held properties.

Is there CGT on inherited Danish property?

The gain on inherited property is calculated from the probate value (arveudlægsværdi), not the original purchase price. There is no CGT on the inheritance itself — only if the heir subsequently sells and realises a gain above the probate value.
