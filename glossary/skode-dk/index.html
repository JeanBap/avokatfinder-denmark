Skode (Deed of Transfer)

A skode is the Danish deed of transfer that legally conveys ownership of real property from seller to buyer. It must be signed by both parties and submitted for tinglysning (registration) to give the buyer full legal title.

The skode is Denmark's equivalent of a title deed or act of sale. It is a formal document that sets out the agreed transfer of ownership — including the property identification (matrikelnummer), names of parties, purchase price, and conditions of transfer. Without a tinglyst (registered) skode, the buyer does not have enforceable ownership rights against third parties.

The skode is typically prepared by the buyer's lawyer (advokat) once the purchase agreement (slutseddel or koebeaftale) has been signed and conditions have been met. It is submitted to the tinglysning system electronically along with the tinglysningsafgift (stamp duty).

Danish property law allows conditional skoder — for example, conditional on a mortgage being granted or on the buyer selling their existing property. The condition must be satisfied within the agreed period, otherwise the skode lapses. The conditional registration is noted in the tinglysning register.

The settlement (berigtigelse) process in Denmark is typically handled by one lawyer — conventionally the buyer's — who also checks title, pays off any existing mortgage, and distributes the net proceeds to the seller. This is different from many countries where separate settlement agents act for each party.

The refundable deposit (deponering) paid by the buyer is typically held in an escrow account and released to the seller only after the tinglysning confirms the buyer's registration. If tinglysning fails (rare but possible due to unresolved encumbrances), the deposit is returned.

Common Mistake

Watch out: In Denmark it is standard for only one lawyer — the buyer's — to handle settlement. This saves cost but means the seller's interests are less actively protected. Sellers in complex transactions should still consider independent legal review.

Expert Tip

Pro tip: Confirm that the skode lists the correct matrikelnummer (land registry parcel number). Errors in the parcel number cause tinglysning rejections and delay completion.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the matrikelnummer?

The unique cadastral parcel identification number assigned to each plot of land in Denmark, maintained by the Danish Geodata Agency. Every skode must reference the correct matrikelnummer.

Can the skode be signed electronically?

Yes. The Danish tinglysning system supports full digital signing via MitID. Physical signatures are no longer required for standard residential transfers.

What happens if the seller has an existing mortgage on the property?

The buyer's lawyer uses part of the purchase funds to redeem the seller's mortgage (indfri pantebreve) as part of the settlement process, ensuring the buyer receives clear title.
