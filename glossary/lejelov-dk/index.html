Lejelov

Lejelov Denmark – Danish Tenancy Law and Tenant Rights

The Lejelov (Danish Tenancy Act — Lejeloven 2022) is the primary legislation governing residential tenancies in Denmark. It consolidates and significantly revised the previous tenancy framework, establishing tenant and landlord rights for private rental housing. Danish tenancy law is one of the most tenant-protective regimes in Europe.

Rent regulation (huslejeregulering) applies to most older private rental housing. The permissible rent is typically the lower of: the cost rent (omkostningsbestemt leje — covering operating costs and a return on the property's assessed value) or the market rent for comparable unregulated properties. Small landlords (renting fewer than seven units in the same building) are partially exempt.

Tenants in regulated properties have strong security of tenure. Landlords can only terminate a regulated tenancy on limited statutory grounds: breach by the tenant, the landlord's own need to occupy, demolition, or renovation that requires the unit to be vacated. Even then, notice periods are long (typically three to six months) and compensation may be required.

2022 reformTightened regulation, transparency requirements

The 2022 Lejelov reform tightened rent regulation for previously exempt categories and introduced new rules on transparency. Landlords must now provide prospective tenants with an itemised rent statement showing how the rent is calculated. Charging above the permitted rent is subject to rent reduction orders by the Huslejenævnet (Rent Tribunal).

Buyers of investment properties in Denmark must understand the regulatory status of each unit: free market (frilejet), regulated by cost rent (omkostningsbestemt leje), or regulated by fair market comparison. The regulatory status directly determines the sustainable rent and the property's income potential.

⚠ Common MistakePurchasing investment property assuming market rents are achievable when the property is subject to rent regulation. Regulated rents can be 30–50% below market in high-demand areas of Copenhagen.
💡 Expert TipBefore purchasing a rental investment, obtain the current rent documentation for each unit and a legal opinion on the regulatory status. The Huslejenævnet's online database shows recent rent tribunal decisions for comparable properties in the area.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is frilejet (free market rent)?

Properties built after 31 December 1991 and single-unit landlords (under seven units) are generally subject to market rent rather than cost regulation.

How long is the standard notice period for a Danish residential tenancy?

Tenants can give one month's notice. Landlords must give three to six months' notice depending on the grounds for termination and tenure length.

Can a landlord raise rent in a regulated property?

Yes, but only within the limits of the lejelov. Rent increases require proper notice (varsel) and can be challenged before the Huslejenævnet within eight weeks of the notice date.
