Ejendomsmaeglerloven (Estate Agent Act)

The Danish Estate Agent Act (ejendomsmaeglerloven) regulates the profession of estate agency, requiring all agents to hold a state licence and imposing mandatory duties to both buyer and seller — including an obligation to disclose all material facts.

Denmark's ejendomsmaeglerloven (Act on Real Estate Agents) requires all practising estate agents (ejendomsmaeglers) to hold a state licence (ejendomsmaeglerbeskikkelse) issued by Erhvervs- og Boligstyrelsen. The licence requires a university-level education in property valuation and law. Unlicensed trading in property on behalf of others is illegal.

A key feature of Danish estate agency law is the duty of equal treatment: the agent has obligations to both buyer and seller, not exclusively to the seller as in many other countries. The agent must not mislead the buyer, must disclose all known material defects, and must present all offers to the seller in good faith.

The typical commission structure charges the seller only — Danish agents do not generally charge a buyer's fee. Commission rates are freely negotiable and typically range from 1–3% of the sale price plus VAT. A fixed fee (fast honorar) is also sometimes agreed for high-value properties.

Agents must prepare a salgsopstilling (property particulars) with specific mandatory disclosures: the tilstandsrapport, elinstallationsrapport, energimaerkning (energy certificate), and ejerudgifter. Omitting any of these documents is a breach of the Act.

Complaints against estate agents are adjudicated by the Klagenvaerket for Ejendomsformidling — the dedicated complaints body. Agents found to have breached the Act face fines, suspension, or loss of licence. This regulatory framework means Danish buyers can generally rely on agents operating within clear professional boundaries.

Common Mistake

Watch out: Assuming the estate agent works only for the seller's benefit is incorrect in Denmark — agents have statutory duties to both parties. However, their primary commercial interest is in completing the sale, so buyers should still conduct independent due diligence.

Expert Tip

Pro tip: Verify the agent's licence status on the Boligsiden or Homes portal before engaging with them. A quick check takes 30 seconds and confirms you are dealing with a regulated professional.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can the seller negotiate commission downwards?

Yes, commission is freely negotiable in Denmark. In slow markets or for high-value properties, rates below 1% are sometimes agreed. Always get the commission in writing before instructing an agent.

What mandatory documents must the agent provide to the buyer?

Tilstandsrapport, elinstallationsrapport, energimaerkning (EPC), ejerudgifter breakdown, and the property's tinglysning extract. Failure to provide these is a breach of the ejendomsmaeglerloven.

What is the Klagenvaerket for Ejendomsformidling?

The statutory complaints tribunal for estate agency disputes. Buyers or sellers who believe an agent has breached the Act can file a complaint free of charge. The tribunal can order compensation and recommend disciplinary action.
