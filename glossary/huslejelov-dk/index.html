Huslejenaevn / Husleje (Rent Review Tribunal and Rent Regulation)

Denmark has extensive rent regulation for private lettings, administered by the Huslejenaevn (rent review tribunal). Landlords in regulated properties cannot freely set or increase rent, making it essential to understand the rules before buying a rental property.

Denmark's rental market is governed by the Lejeloven (Rent Act) and the Boligreguleringsloven (Housing Regulation Act), which together impose strict rent controls on most private rental properties in larger municipalities. Landlords who own regulated properties (those built before 1991 in municipalities with more than 20,000 inhabitants) cannot charge market rent — they must charge 'cost-based' rent (omkostningsbestemt leje) or 'value rent' (den lejedes vaerdi), depending on the property type.

The Huslejenaevn (municipal rent review tribunal) adjudicates disputes between landlords and tenants over rent levels, maintenance obligations, deposits, and notice periods. Tenants can file a complaint if they believe their rent exceeds the legally permitted level. If successful, the landlord must reduce the rent and repay excess rent collected — sometimes retroactively for years.

Post-2015, the rules have been somewhat liberalised: properties that have been substantially refurbished (gennemgribende modernisering) can charge higher rent under a different regime. However, the legal definition of substantial refurbishment is strict and requires documented expenditure above a statutory threshold per square metre.

Properties built after 1991 (or first let after 31 December 1991) are largely exempt from rent regulation and can be let at market rent. This is a critical distinction for investors — pre-1991 properties are fundamentally different investment assets from post-1991 ones.

For investors considering a purchase of a tenanted property (udlejet fast ejendom), understanding the existing tenancy's legal basis, the applicable rent regime, and the tenant's statutory rights is essential before agreeing a price.

Common Mistake

Watch out: Buying a pre-1991 rental property assuming you can raise rents to market level is a fundamental miscalculation. Research the applicable rent regime — and get a lawyer's opinion on the current tenant's legal rent — before any offer.

Expert Tip

Pro tip: When buying a tenanted property, always obtain the full tenancy file: lease, deposit receipt, rent payment history, and any Huslejenaevn correspondence. Hidden tribunal complaints or historical rent disputes can expose you to significant liability.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is 'omkostningsbestemt leje'?

Cost-based rent calculated from the building's running costs (maintenance, insurance, administration, and return on investment). It is typically well below market rent for regulated properties in Copenhagen.

Can I evict a sitting tenant easily in Denmark?

No. Danish tenancy law provides very strong tenant protection. Eviction is only permitted on specific statutory grounds (non-payment, serious breach). Buying a tenanted property effectively means inheriting the tenant long-term.

Does rent regulation apply to holiday lets?

No. Short-term tourist lets are not subject to the Boligreguleringsloven. However, municipalities have introduced local rules restricting short-term lets in residential areas — check local bylaws before relying on Airbnb income.
